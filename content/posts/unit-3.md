 This unit was on reflecting on our failures. This lab for Unit 3 — Reflecting on Failure was different. Not just because we were writing about failure instead of trying to fix a broken pipeline or debug a 
 conflict but because for once failure wasn’t something to analyze and to work on rather than hiding the msitakes. It was the whole point.
            Our group was Group 9, and we were assigned two things;

           1.The Mars Orbiter failure case study

             2.The Prologue + Chapter 8 of Amy Edmondson’s Right Kind of Wrong: The Science of Failing Well

    I personally worked on the reflection part for the Mars Orbiter and contributed to the chapter reflections as well. And honestly digging into a $125 million crash caused by unit mismatch 
    (pounds vs. newton)shift.


        Mars Orbiter:
 The Mars Climate Orbiter didn’t explode because of bad code or lazy engineers. It failed because one team used pounds-force, another used newtons, and nobody thought to double-check between systems.
 In GitBash, I’ve had my own "pounds vs. newtons" moments. Like the time I deleted a whole branch I thought I had merged except I didn’t or when I typed git push force without really understanding. Small,
 silent mismatches between what I thought would happen and what actually happened.
 The Mars Orbiter didn’t fail because people were lazy. It failed because they trusted the system too much.

       Right Kind of Wrong: What I Learned from Chapter 8
Amy Edmondson’s book separates failure into three types:

Basic (stupid mistakes)

Complex (systems fail in unexpected ways)

 Intelligent (good experiments that just didn’t work).

 The Mars Orbiter? Complex failure all the way. No single person was to blame. The system the communication between teams,the assumptions,the lack of verification. What was the result ? Failure.
 Reading Chapter 8, I kept thinking about our own group wiki. We had to coordinate case study, book summary, videos, resources, and individual contributions. One of us misunderstood the deadline format.Another
 thought the video gallery went under “Resources.” lack of trust in between group mates, bad communication, Small misalignments. Not Mars-level expensive, but still tiny pounds-vs-newtons moments.
 The book argues that complex failures deserve curiosity, not blame. And for the first time I actually believed this theory.
      
      What Surprised Me Most-
 Two things:

 The Mars Orbiter’s consequence wasn’t just money lost. It was trust lost. Public trust. Scientific trust. That’s way harder to rebuild than a rocket I feel.

 Edmondson says most teams hide complex failures because they’re afraid of looking incompetent. But hiding them guarantees they’ll happen again.

 In GitBash labs, I’ve definitely hidden my mistake and by pretending it wasn’t helpful in learning at all.     

   Takeaway-

   This wiki project didn’t ask us to be perfect. It asked us to be honest. And honestly? That was harder than writing 500 words or formatting a wiki page as a group because of reasons.
I’m glad I got the reflection part of the Mars Orbiter. It made me look at my own GitBash fails differently not as embarrassments, but as data. Small, cheap, recoverable versions of the same patterns 
misalignment,assumption.So yeah, Unit 3 wasn’t about fixing failure. It was about learning to talk about it. And that’s a skill I’ll actually use.
